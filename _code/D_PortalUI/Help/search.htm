General Search box: Enables you to do a single or multiple word search.

  • Enter the word(s) you want to search for and click on the arrow  button.
  • A list of results will be displayed. Click on the title of the desired result to view the text of the document.

Advanced Search: Enables you to perform a focused search by selecting from a list of options.

  • Enter the word or phrase that you want to search for.
  • Select whether you want to search for "All Words," "Any Words," "Exact Phrase," or "Boolean Search".
    • All Words - Each word must be present in the document to return a hit result.
    • Any Words - At least one of the words must be present to return a hit result.
    • Exact Phrase - All words must be present in the same order that you have typed them in to return a hit result.
    • Boolean Search - Allows you to refine your queries to focus on more specific or more general information than may be found with a single word or phrase search. Click here for more details on Boolean searching.
  • Select whether you want to show excerpts in context of the document in your search results. If you do not want excerpts to display, de-select the box to the left of Show Excerpts.
  • Select whether you want to search all AICPA Online Publications or just those you subscribe to. To include documents not included in your current subscription, select the box to the left of Search Unsubscribed Content.
  • Click on the GO button to perform the search.
  • A list of results will be displayed. Click on the title of the desired result to view the text of the document.

Boolean Operator Searches (And, Or, Not, XOr)

The Boolean operators allow you to refine your queries to focus on more specific or more general information than may be found with a single word or phrase search.

Boolean operators require a word or phrase on either side of the operator. For example, dog or cat is a valid query, but or cat dog is not. The exception is the not operator. The not operator can be used before a term and still be valid. For example, not cat dog returns the same result as dog not cat.There are four Boolean operators: And, Or, Not, XOr (Exclusive Or). See Operator Precedence for a discussion of the precedence (order of execution) of these operators and how to override the precedence.

And
Use the And operator to search for multiple words in the same document.

The And operator may be specified any one of three ways: a single space between words, the ampersand &, or the word and.

Query Explanation
view codes
view & codes
view and codes 		Finds all documents which contain both view and codes.
accountant & attorney and doctor Finds all documents which contain accountant, attorney, and doctor.

Or

Use the Or operator to search for multiple words in the document collection (the words may or may not be in the same document).

The Or operator may be specified one of two ways: the pipe | or the word or.

Query Explanation
view | codes
view or codes 		Finds all documents which contain either view or codes (may contain both)
accountant or attorney and doctor Finds all documents which contain accountant and either attorney or doctor (or both attorney and doctor). (See Operator Precedence for additional explanation on these results.)

Not
Use the Not operator to search for documents which do not contain a particular term or set of terms. Not is actually a unary operator (it can be used by itself), but it is often used in between two terms (find documents which contain A but not B).

Generally, the best way to think of the Not operator when used between two terms (A not B) is "find documents with A and find documents without B" (A and not B).

The Not operator may be specified one of two ways: the caret ^ or the word not.

Query Explanation
view ^ codes
view not codes 		Finds all documents which contain view but which do not contain codes.
^ account
not account 		Finds all documents which do not contain account.


Operator Precedence
The query operators have a precedence order. That is, when two more operators are used in a query, the results of one operator will be evaluated before another operator. Understanding the precedence -- and how to override the precedence -- can assist you in finding the information you need.

The operator precedence is: Not, And, Or
If the same operators are used in a query (for example, if Or appears twice in a query), the operators are evaluated from left-to-right.

These rules allow you to create powerful queries; however, they can also be confusing. To ensure the correct terms are combined with the correct operators, use parentheses around the terms and operators.


Wildcard Searches

Wildcards allow you to find words using patterns for a set of words (Single Character or Multiple Character) and to find synonyms or forms of a word.

Note that wildcards may be used in phrase and proximity searches.


Single Character
Use the single character wildcard to replace any single character in a term. Note that this wildcard is a replacement wildcard -- there must be a character in the wildcard position to register a hit.

Two or more single character wildcards may be used in the same term, if necessary.

The single character wildcard is the question mark ?.

Query Explanation
wom?n Finds all terms which match the pattern (such as woman, women, or wom9n).
b??k Finds all four letter words which start with b and end with k (such as book, bilk, or bark).
"the best advi?e" Finds all phrases which match the pattern (such as "the best advise" or "the best advice").

Multiple Character

Use the multiple character wildcard to replace 0 or more characters in a term.

Two or more multiple character wildcards may be used in the same term, if necessary.

The multiple character wildcard is the asterisk (or star) *.

Query Explanation
work* Finds all terms which start with the pattern work (such as work, worked, or workhorse).
*ed Finds all terms which end in ed (such as worked, overloaded, or red).
“gross* negligen*” Finds all documents which contain phrases which match the pattern (such as "gross negligence" or "grossly negligent").



Viewing Your Search Results

After performing a search, the search results will be displayed below the search form. The search results display the path to the document, document title, and document excerpts with your search words displayed in bold type.  To view a document, click on the document title.

Refining Your Search

  • To refine your search, open the Refine Search Dimensions window by clicking link or the the arrow.

    •       The content hierarchy will be displayed. Navigate down the hierarchy to refine your search. As you move down the hierarchy, the search results will automatically reflect the reduction in your search scope.

    To remove the refinement of the search results, click the remove link at the top right of the Selected Search Dimensions.



    Modifying and Clearing Your Search

    To modify your search, enter your new search words and options in the form above the search results and click the GO button.

    To clear your search, click the clear search link on the search form.
